A computer 'looks' at a photo of a cat. What does it actually receive?

Every digital image is just a grid of numbers (0 = black … 255 = white, ×3 for color). Everything a computer 'sees' — edges, whiskers, the cat — must somehow be computed from that grid of numbers. Today's pattern is THE trick for doing it.

Concept 1 · the raw material

An image is a grid of numbers

  • A grayscale photo is a table of brightness values: 0 = black, 255 = white. A 1000×1000 photo = a million numbers.
  • Color = three grids stacked (red, green, blue channels).
  • Nowhere in those numbers does it say "edge", "whisker", or "cat" — meaning has to be computed.
  • Question of the day: what computation turns pixels into meaning?

Concept 2 · the pattern

Convolution = a sliding window with weights

Take a tiny grid of weights — a kernel (say 3×3). Slide it across the image. At every stop: multiply each pixel by its weight, add them up, write one number down.

Placekernel over a 3×3 patch
Multiplypixel × weight, all 9 pairs
Sum→ one output number
Slideone pixel over, repeat
Pattern series connection: Pattern 01 was "keep only the best K." Pattern 02 is "slide a small window over big data" — the same sliding-window idea behind moving averages and (spoiler) how attention scans text. A high output number means "the thing this kernel describes is HERE."
The kernel is a tiny pattern-template: it outputs a big number where the image locally matches it. Don't belabor the arithmetic here — the next slide lets them watch and drive it.

Check · after the animation

Quick check · tap an answer

The vertical-edge kernel slides over a region where ALL pixels have the same brightness. What does it output there?

The kernel's left column adds the patch, the right column subtracts it. If the patch is uniform, they cancel: output 0. The kernel only 'fires' where brightness CHANGES left-to-right — that's why it's an edge detector.

If they miss this, go back one slide, paint a solid block, and step the window through the middle of it.
Concept 3 · stack it deep

A CNN = layers of learned kernels

A Convolutional Neural Network is that animation, repeated: convolve → keep what fired → convolve the result again, dozens of layers deep.

Early layers

Kernels that fire on edges & lines — like the ones you just played with.

Middle layers

Combine edges into textures & parts: fur, an eye, a wheel, a corner.

Late layers

Combine parts into whole objects: "cat 0.96 · dog 0.03".

The twist that makes it AI: nobody writes the kernel numbers. They start random, and training tunes them — the network invents its own edge detectors because they're genuinely useful for the task. That's the "learning" in deep learning.
Two big ideas on one slide: hierarchy (edges→parts→objects) and learned-not-hand-written kernels. The 1998 LeNet → 2012 AlexNet story fits here if there's time: same idea, more layers + more data + GPUs.

Where CNNs hide · part 1

You used a CNN before breakfast

📱 Face unlock

A CNN turns your face into a signature of numbers and matches it — edges → features → "it's you", in milliseconds, on the phone itself.

📷 Portrait mode

A CNN labels every pixel person / background so the camera knows what to blur. Night mode & sharpening: convolutions too.

🔎 Photo search

Type "dog" into your photos app — a CNN already tagged every image. No human ever labeled your library.

The other classic · your question

And what's an RNN?

CNNs are built for grids (images). A Recurrent Neural Network is built for sequences — text, audio, anything that arrives in order.

Read one stepa word / audio slice
Update memorythe "hidden state"
Carry it forwardrepeat for the next step

Like reading a sentence: you don't re-read from the start at every word — you carry a running summary in your head. That summary is the RNN's hidden state.

Two patterns, two data shapes: CNN = a window sliding over space. RNN = a memory running over time. Before 2017, RNNs powered translation, speech recognition, and autocomplete.
This slide + the next one are the direct answer to the Class-02 student question "what are CNN and RNN?". Keep the mechanism intuitive: read → update memory → carry forward. LSTM/GRU are refinements of the same idea — name-drop only if asked.
Plot twist · why you hear less about RNNs now

Why Transformers replaced RNNs

RNN's two weaknesses

1 · One step at a time. Word 500 can't be processed before words 1–499 — painfully slow to train on GPUs built for parallel work.
2 · Long-distance forgetting. By the end of a long paragraph, the running memory has squeezed out the beginning.

Attention's fix (2017)

Let every word look at every other word directly — all at once, no running memory, fully parallel. The paper: "Attention Is All You Need."

The result is the Transformer — the T in ChatGPT. RNNs still matter (and their ideas echo in newer models), but attention won the language war. How attention scans a sequence is exactly Pattern 03 — next in this series.
This gives the honest modern picture: student asks about RNNs in 2026, the follow-up is always "so why does everyone say transformer?". Sets up Pattern 03 (two pointers & sliding window → attention) as a cliffhanger.
Connect it · a researcher's instinct

CNN or RNN? Look at the shape of your data

Grid-shaped?

Photos, medical scans, game boards, spectrograms → CNN (nearby pixels relate).

Sequence-shaped?

Text, audio, sensor streams, DNA → RNN family, or today a Transformer.

Both / unusual?

Video = grid + time. Molecules = graphs. Architectures mix & match — the data's shape decides.

This instinct — "what's the shape of my data?" — is a genuine research skill. You'll use it when you build your first neural network in AI Toolbox Class 13, and in every project after.
Elevates the lesson from trivia to a transferable decision rule. Modern caveat if asked: Vision Transformers now do images too — the boundary blurs, but data-shape-first thinking still holds.

Wrap-up

Three things to keep

  • An image is a grid of numbers — a convolution slides a small weighted window over it, and fires where the image matches the kernel's pattern
  • A CNN stacks learned kernels: edges → textures → parts → objects. Nobody writes the kernels — training does
  • CNN for grids, RNN for sequences — with attention/Transformers as the modern heir to RNNs
