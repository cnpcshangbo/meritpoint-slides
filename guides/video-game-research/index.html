Scientists study video games for real: what makes them fun, why some sell 80 million copies and others 80, whether practice in a game transfers to real skills. You can do this kind of research too — because the game industry publishes mountains of free data. These notes help you pick a question, grab a real dataset, and run the same pipeline we use in class. You do not need to be a math genius. You need to be curious about games — and you already are.

1What makes a question a research question?

There is a simple test. A research question is one that data can answer. Opinions can't be measured; numbers can:

QuestionResearch?Why
"Is Zelda the best game ever?"❌ Opinion"Best" means something different to everyone — no data can settle it.
"Why are games fun?"❌ Too bigA great mystery, but too huge to measure in one project.
"Do Racing games sell more than Puzzle games?"✅ Yes!Two groups, one number (sales) — data answers this directly.
"Do games with higher critic scores sell more copies?"✅ Yes!Two numbers per game — you can check if they move together.
"Did game prices on Steam change after 2020?"✅ Yes!Compare before vs after — a classic research shape.
The magic pattern: almost every beginner research question has the shape "Is ___ related to ___?" or "Does group A have more ___ than group B?" If your question fits one of those shapes, you can research it. If it doesn't, reshape it until it does.

Quiz — which one could YOU answer with a spreadsheet of game data?

Only the middle one compares measurable numbers between clear groups. The other two are great debates — but debates, not data questions.

2Build your research question 🛠️

Pick two ingredients — the builder writes the question and tells you which dataset can answer it:

Is a game's  related to its  ?
Then sharpen it. "Is genre related to sales?" is good; "Did Sports games outsell Action games on the Wii?" is better — smaller questions give sharper answers. Professional scientists shrink their questions on purpose.

5The pipeline — same 6 steps as class

Every research project in our course walks the same road: Question → Data → Look → Analyze → Chart → Conclusion. Here it is for the worked example "Which genres sell most?" — swap in your own question and dataset:

import pandas as pd

df = pd.read_csv("/Users/you/data/vgsales.csv")   # ← YOUR path here (Guide 01, section 2!)
df.head()                    # LOOK: first 5 rows — always eyeball your data first
df.shape                     # how many games? (rows, columns)
# ANALYZE: total sales per genre, biggest first
totals = df.groupby("Genre")["Global_Sales"].sum().sort_values(ascending=False)
totals
# CHART: one line makes the picture
totals.plot(kind="barh", title="Total sales by genre (millions)")
Then be an honest scientist — add one more sentence: "But my data can't see ___." (digital sales? mobile games? games after 2016?) Every real paper has a limitations section. Yours should too. It doesn't weaken your project — it's what makes it science.

Quiz — your data shows games with more Twitch viewers also have more sales. Does watching cause buying?

Two things moving together ("correlation") doesn't prove one causes the other. Maybe streaming sells games; maybe great games attract streamers; maybe both. Saying "these are related, and here are three possible reasons" is EXACTLY what good scientists write.
