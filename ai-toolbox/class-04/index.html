Concept 1 · what it actually is

One tool, five hats

"Chatbot" undersells it. An LLM is a text-processing engine that plays whatever role you assign — and each role helps a different pipeline stage:

📚 Researcher

Summarizes papers, explains concepts in plain words. (Question & data stages.)

💻 Programmer

Writes, explains, and debugs code — you've used this one: the labs' ✨ assistant. (Model stage.)

📊 Data analyst

Suggests analyses, writes chart code, interprets results. (Data & experiment stages.)

🧑‍🏫 Mentor

Critiques your plan, spots gaps, suggests directions when you're stuck. (Every stage.)

✍️ Writing partner

Drafts outlines and abstracts, polishes academic prose. (Conclusion stage.)

🎩 You pick the hat

The role doesn't switch itself — your prompt assigns it. That's the skill we learn next.

Each hat maps to a stage of Class 3's pipeline — students should annotate their own diagram with where they'd use which hat. The sixth box is the segue: role assignment is prompt engineering.
Concept 2 · the idea in one picture

Anatomy of a research prompt

Vague in, vague out. Four parts turn a wish into an instruction:

1
Role"You are a research assistant…"
2
Context"Here is the paper's abstract: …"
3
Format"Reply in 3 bullets: summary · contribution · limitation."
4
Constraints"Quote the exact sentence behind each claim."
Result ▸ a useful, checkable answer — the quoted sentences are your verification handles.
KEY IDEA — a good prompt names the exact format you want the answer in. The constraints part is dashed for a reason: it's the piece almost everyone forgets, and it's the piece that makes answers verifiable.
The signature visual of the hour — same layout language as Class 2's funnel and Class 3's lifecycle. 'Checkable' is the bridge between the prompting half and the verification half of the class: constraints like 'quote the sentence' exist FOR verification.
Prompt upgrade 1 of 3

Vague → specific

❌ Vague

You ▸ Explain AI.

No audience, no scope, no format → a generic essay that helps nobody.

✅ Specific

You ▸ Explain artificial intelligence to a 10-year-old, using two everyday analogies, in under 100 words.

Audience + format + length → a targeted, vivid, usable answer.

The move: answer who, what, why, how inside your prompt. Sound familiar? It's the question ladder from Class 2 — vague → specific — applied to prompts. Same skill, new target.
The Class-2 callback does real work: students already trained this exact muscle on research questions. Run the upgrade live if time — the quality jump sells itself.
Prompt upgrade 2 of 3

Assign the hat: role-playing

❌ No role

You ▸ How do I write a linear-regression function in Python?

You'll get bare code — no reasoning, no guidance, no warnings.

✅ With a role

You ▸ Act as a patient data-science tutor. Teach a beginner to write linear regression with NumPy only: explain the idea first, then the code with line comments, then one common mistake.

Now it teaches: background, commented code, pitfalls — like a tutor, not a search engine.

The move: start with "Act as…" or "You are…". The role sets depth, tone, and what the model volunteers unasked — that's the difference between passive Q&A and proactive teaching.
Connects back to the five hats: role-play is HOW you put a hat on the model. The upgraded prompt also sneak-previews Class 10's linear regression.
Prompt upgrade 3 of 3

Show, don't describe: give an example

❌ Describing the style

You ▸ Summarize this paragraph in a creative way.

"Creative" means a thousand things — you'll get a lottery ticket: maybe a poem, maybe a story, rarely what you pictured.

✅ Showing the style

You ▸ Summarize it as a haiku. Example — Input: "The quick brown fox…" Output: "Fox leaps, dog dreams on, / a chase painted in the air, / nature's brief ballet."

One example anchors format, tone, and length in a single shot.

The move: when you need a specific format or style, paste one worked example. (The pros call this "few-shot prompting" — you just learned it.) Your homework's 3-part paper summary? Give it one example summary and watch the consistency jump.
Few-shot named casually so the vocabulary lands without ceremony. The homework tie-in is practical: an example SCL summary in the prompt yields uniform structure across papers.

Concept 3 · the paper-summary framework

Three things matter about any paper

📄 Summary

What they did, in plain words. One or two sentences a classmate would understand.

✨ Contribution

What's actually new. Not "they studied X" — what can we do now that we couldn't before?

🕳️ Limitation

Where it falls short — the scenario not covered, the data not tested. This is where your project ideas live.

You ▸ You are a research assistant. Here is a paper's abstract: […]. Reply in exactly 3 bullets — summary, contribution, limitation — and quote the sentence that supports each.
Remember Class 2's secret? "Future work" sections are free research questions. A paper's limitation is the same gold from the other side — and now an LLM mines it for you in seconds. Then you verify.
The framework IS the deliverable and the graded homework. The full prompt on-slide combines all four anatomy parts — point at each. Limitation-mining closes the loop with Class 2's gap-hunting method.

Concept 4 · the failure mode

Hallucination: confident nonsense

What it is: the model generates plausible-sounding but false information — fake studies, fake numbers, fake people — in a perfectly confident tone.

Why it happens: an LLM doesn't look facts up — it predicts the next likely word, over and over. When the truth isn't strongly in its training data, the most plausible continuation wins. Grammar: perfect. Facts: optional.

🚩 High-risk zones

  • Specific citations & paper titles
  • Precise numbers and statistics
  • Names you've never heard of
  • Anything "too good to be true"
The rule: never cite anything you haven't opened yourself. If you can't find the source, it doesn't exist for your purposes — no matter how real it sounds. You'll build this exact mechanism in Lab 1 and see why it can't help itself.
The mechanical explanation (next-word prediction) matters more than the warning — it converts 'AI sometimes lies' into 'AI always predicts; sometimes prediction and truth diverge.' Lab 1 makes it visceral.
Case study · a hallucination in the wild

Meet Dr. Alice Smith (who doesn't exist)

You ▸ Tell me about the 2025 quantum-computing breakthrough by Dr. Alice Smith.
AI ▸ Dr. Alice Smith, a top researcher at MIT, announced a major breakthrough in early 2025. Her team built a 1000-qubit computer, achieving quantum supremacy — solving in 3 hours a problem that would take classical supercomputers 10,000 years. Published in Nature, March 2025.

🚩 Red flag 1

Hyper-specific details — 1000 qubits, 3 hours, 10,000 years — for an event you can't find anywhere.

🚩 Red flag 2

A named researcher + institution + journal — all unverifiable. The question invited the invention.

✅ The check

Search "Alice Smith MIT quantum 2025" in Scholar/news. Zero authoritative hits → hallucination. Time: 90 seconds.

Note the subtle lesson: the USER's leading question planted the premise, and the model obligingly built a world around it. LLMs rarely push back on false premises — they complete them. The 90-second check is the discussion slide's warm-up.
Check · spot the mechanism

Quick check · tap an answer

Why does an LLM state fake citations in exactly the same confident tone as real ones?

One mechanism produces everything: next-word prediction. Real facts and inventions come out of the same pipe with the same fluency — which is why tone tells you nothing and opening the source tells you everything.

If students can articulate this, the verify habit has a foundation instead of being a rule to memorize. Lab 1 next makes them build the pipe.

The 2-minute verify, live

Close the loop: take the quoted sentences from demo 1 and hunt them down in the real world.

You ▸ Check the claimed contribution against the paper's own abstract — did the LLM overstate it?
You ▸ Verdict per claim: ✓ verified · ✗ wrong · ? can't confirm → treat ? as ✗.

Benchmark · judge against this

What a verified summary looks like

Paper: "YOLO: unified real-time object detection" (Redmon et al.) — our AV case's ancestor.

📄 Summary ✓

Frames detection as a single regression from image to boxes + class probabilities — one network pass instead of a multi-stage pipeline. ✓ matches abstract, sentence 2.

✨ Contribution ✓

Real-time speed (45+ fps) with one unified model — detection fast enough for live video. ✓ verified in paper's §1 and table 1.

🕳️ Limitation ✓

Struggles with small objects in groups (flocks, crowds) — spatial constraints limit nearby detections. ✓ paper's own §2 admits this.

Mined question: "Do crowd-specific training images raise small-pedestrian recall in groups, without hurting overall accuracy?" — input, output, metric, trade-off guard. From paper to project in one hour. (Notice: every ✓ cites where it was checked.)
A real paper, real limitation, and the mined question deliberately echoes Class 2's strong-question shape. The '✓ + where' pattern is the grading bar for homework. YOLO also links back to the Class 1 notebook and forward to the AV thread.
Reference · key terms

Today's vocabulary

LLM — large language model: a next-word predictor trained on vast text (ChatGPT, Claude).

Prompt engineering — designing the input to get research-grade output: role + context + format + constraints.

Role prompt — "Act as…": assigns the hat, sets depth and tone.

Few-shot example — a worked example inside the prompt that anchors format and style.

Hallucination — plausible, confident, false output; a natural by-product of next-word prediction.

Cross-validation (of claims) — confirming AI output against independent, authoritative sources before use.

Note 'cross-validation' here is claim-checking; the ML resampling technique with the same name arrives in Class 14 — flag the collision now to prevent later confusion.

Backup · worked example

The mentor hat, in action

You ▸ Act as a research advisor. My project: predicting student exam scores from study hours and past grades with linear regression. Critique my plan — what am I missing?
AI ▸ Three gaps: (1) Features — hours + grades is thin; consider attendance, sleep, extracurriculars. (2) Baseline & iteration — start with linear regression as baseline, then test random forest / gradient boosting and compare honestly. (3) Evaluation — you haven't said how you'll split data or which error metric decides success.

Accept ✓

Baseline-first and the split critique — straight from Class 3's playbook. It's right.

Accept with care ⚠

More features — but sleep data may be uncollectable. Feasibility is your call, not the model's.

Push back ✗

If it suggests deep learning for 200 rows of data — overkill. You know your scope; it doesn't.

Models homework task 3 end-to-end: solicit criticism, then triage it — accept / adapt / reject with reasons. The triage row is the skill; students who accept everything an LLM says have learned nothing about judgment.
Backup · bonus check

Quick check · tap an answer

Your LLM produces a beautiful summary of a paper, with quoted sentences. You can't find one quoted sentence anywhere in the PDF. What do you do?

One fabricated quote doesn't poison everything, but it does exactly two things: kills that claim, and lowers your trust in its neighbors. Verify locally, salvage what checks out — that's proportionate skepticism, the researcher's default mode.

Proportionate response is the mature skill: neither blind trust (A/B) nor panic (D). 'Models invent direct quotes too' surprises students — worth saying plainly.
