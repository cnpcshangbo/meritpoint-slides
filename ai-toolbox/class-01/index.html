The Story

A summer that became 70 years

In 1956, ten scientists gathered at Dartmouth for what they expected to be a single summer's work: build a machine that could "use language, form abstractions, and solve problems reserved for humans." They coined the name "artificial intelligence" — and wildly underestimated the problem. Three years later, Arthur Samuel wrote a checkers program that got better the more it played against itself. He called what it did "machine learning."
Neural voice · click to play / pause

The founders thought intelligence was a summer project. Samuel's self-improving checkers player was the first hint: don't program intelligence — let a machine learn from examples.

Tell it conversationally. The trick isn't to PROGRAM intelligence but to let a machine LEARN from examples — that's ML. Use the Speak button to let the room just listen. Bridge to Arthur Samuel.
Origin story · 1959 · the first "machine learning"

The program that taught itself checkers

Arthur Samuel, an engineer at IBM, built a checkers (draughts) program on the room-sized IBM 701 — and did something radical: he let it improve on its own.

  • It played thousands of games against itself, remembering which positions led to wins.
  • It tuned its own scoring of board positions — an early ancestor of today's reinforcement learning.
  • Samuel coined the term "machine learning" to describe it (1959).
  • By 1962 it beat a strong human amateur — an early, very public win for AI.

Extended history · the long road

70 years of winters and springs

AI didn't arrive overnight. It boomed, froze, and boomed again — each cycle banking hard-won lessons.

1956
Dartmouth meetingThe term "artificial intelligence" is coined — the field is born.
1970s
First AI winterToo little compute and data; early systems stall, funding dries up.
1980s
Expert systemsRule-based systems find real business use — a short, bright spring.
1990s
Second AI winterExpert systems are costly and brittle; enthusiasm cools again.
2010s+
Big data + deep learningCompute + huge datasets ignite neural nets — today's golden era.
Knowing this history keeps you level-headed about hype: every boom looked unstoppable, and every winter looked permanent. Today is a real boom — but ask what's hype.
From the Chinese deck's history slide. Two winters, two springs. The lesson is calibration: neither doom nor hype. Deep learning's spark was data + compute, not just cleverer math.

Concept 1

AI ⊃ Machine Learning ⊃ Deep Learning

  • AI — the whole field of machine intelligence
  • ML — systems that improve from data, not hand-written rules
  • DL — machine learning using many-layered neural networks
KEY IDEA — When people say "AI" today, they usually mean machine learning. Deep Blue was AI but not ML; ChatGPT is all three.
Nested circles, not synonyms. Every DL system is ML; every ML system is AI — not the reverse.
Concept 2 · what powers each layer

Different tools at each ring

AI

Broadest vision. Less data-driven. Key tech: expert systems, search algorithms. (Deep Blue lives here.)

ML

Learns from data. Tunes a model's parameters. Key tech: decision trees, SVMs. Used for prediction & classification.

DL

Neural networks. Needs big data + compute. Key tech: CNNs, RNNs, Transformers. Powers vision & language.

AI is the grand vision · ML is the path that got us there · DL is ML's strongest tool for hard, messy data.
From the Chinese deck. Don't drill the acronyms — the point is each ring uses different machinery, and DL's rise is what made the 2010s boom.

What YOU actually learn

Two skills this workflow trains

The Skill of Asking
Choosing the right question

Picking and sharpening the right question (steps 1–2). A good question is specific, measurable, and answerable — it decides everything downstream.

The Skill of Judging
Telling good work from weak

Reading a draft (steps 6–8) and saying precisely what's strong, what's wrong, and what to fix — taste you can defend.

AI can draft for you. It cannot ask your question or judge your taste — those stay human, and they're what we grade.
The heart of the pedagogy. Machine handles production; the student owns asking and judging. Both are transferable life skills.

Case study · our running example

Teaching a car to see

Autonomous driving threads the whole course. The car's AI splits into three jobs — and this course lives in the first one.

1 · Perception

See the world — detect pedestrians, cars, signs from camera images. (CNNs)

2 · Prediction

Forecast where each object moves next. (RNNs / Transformers)

3 · Planning

Decide: brake, steer, or keep going. (reinforcement learning)

Perception is the foundation: if the car sees wrong, perfect prediction and planning can't keep it safe.

Reference · key terms

Today's vocabulary

AI — machines doing tasks that normally need human intelligence.

Machine Learning — systems that learn patterns from data instead of explicit rules.

Deep Learning — ML with many-layered neural networks for complex data.

Perception — turning sensor input (images, sound) into understanding.

Prediction — using a trained model to estimate future or unknown values.

Planning — choosing a sequence of actions to reach a goal.
