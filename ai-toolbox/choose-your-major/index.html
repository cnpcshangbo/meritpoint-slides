Why this matters now

A research topic is evidence, not decoration

Selective universities don't just ask "are you smart?" They ask "what will you study here, and why should we believe you?" Almost every top application has a "why this major" essay or an intended field of study.

Say it

"I want to study neuroscience." — anyone can write this sentence.

Show it

"I ran a study measuring reaction-time and attention." — now the claim has a spine.

Aim it

Pick the major first, and your research topic can point straight at it — one story, not two.

The loop of this hour: choose a major direction → let it aim your course research topic → the finished project becomes the strongest evidence that you belong in that major. This is why we're spending a class on majors inside a research course.
Land the reframe: the research project they build in this course is not separate from admissions — it IS the admissions artifact, if they aim it. Chinese families often treat "activities" and "academics" as separate lists; the point here is to fuse them.
Vocabulary · say it right

Major, concentration, minor — what's the difference?

Major = Concentration 主修专业

Your main field — most of your advanced courses and your senior thesis live here. Princeton calls it a concentration. You usually declare it at the end of sophomore year, but your application already signals the direction.

Minor / Certificate 辅修 / 证书

A smaller, focused add-on — often interdisciplinary and open to any major. Example: Princeton's Statistics & Machine Learning (SML) minor lets a Politics or Biology major add real data skills.

Two Princeton degrees: the A.B. (Bachelor of Arts — humanities, social sciences, most sciences) and the B.S.E. (Bachelor of Science in Engineering). Computer Science is offered as both — same field, two flavors. In total Princeton has 37 concentrations and 50+ minors & certificates.
Get the words right so students sound informed when they talk to parents and counselors. Emphasize the SML minor: it's the perfect bridge for this course's students — you can major in almost anything and still formally certify the AI/data skills you're building right now.

Zoom in · for AI Toolbox students

Majors your AI research can point at

The data & ML skills you're building in this course support many majors — not just Computer Science. For each, a sample research topic you could actually do this term:

Computer Science

Studies: algorithms, ML, systems. Topic ▸ "Can a small CNN read handwritten digits as well as a big one?"

Oper. Research & Fin. Eng. (ORFE)

Studies: optimization, data, risk. Topic ▸ "Which features best predict bike-share demand at rush hour?"

Neuroscience

Studies: brain + computation. Topic ▸ "Do reaction-time patterns predict attention lapses?"

Molecular Biology

Studies: genes, cells, data. Topic ▸ "Can gene-expression clusters separate two tissue types?"

Economics

Studies: markets, causal data. Topic ▸ "How does study time relate to test scores across schools?"

Psychology

Studies: behavior + statistics. Topic ▸ "Does background music change reading-comprehension scores?"

And the umbrella: whatever you major in, the Statistics & Machine Learning minor formally certifies the exact skills this course teaches. Your project can be the "substantial application of machine learning" that minor requires.
This is the money slide for these students: AI is not one major, it's a method that plugs into a dozen majors. A biology-loving student does not have to become a CS major to use ML — they can do Molecular Biology + SML minor and let their research topic prove both. Quick check · strategy

Quick check · tap an answer

You love medicine and you're building a machine-learning project in this course. Which plan makes the strongest college application?

The power move is coherence: a health-facing major PLUS a research topic aimed at a health question tells one convincing story. AI is a method you carry into the major — you rarely have to abandon the subject you love to use it. 'Keep options open' reads as 'no direction' to an admissions reader.

Students (and parents) often think loving AI means majoring in CS. Break that: the strongest applications are coherent, not maximally 'prestigious'. One interest, aimed research, matching major.

Widen the lens · beyond one school

The same interest, many names

Princeton is our worked example, but every university slices majors differently. Learn to compare — the label changes, the field doesn't:

MIT

Names majors by number — Course 6 is Electrical Eng. & Computer Science, and 6-9 is Computation & Cognition (CS + brain).

Stanford

Has CS with an AI track, plus Symbolic Systems — a blend of CS, linguistics, psychology & philosophy.

Carnegie Mellon

A whole School of Computer Science, and the first US undergraduate B.S. in Artificial Intelligence.

Homework habit: for any dream school, find its "majors / fields of study" page and locate the 2–3 that fit your interest. The names differ; your job is to match the content, not the label.
Point: don't fixate on one school's vocabulary. The skill is reading any university's fields-of-study page and mapping it to your interest. These three examples are accurate and stable; encourage students to go verify each on the school's own site as practice.
The hard part · talk to your parents

The parent conversation · 和父母的对话

This decision is a family one. Come prepared, not defensive. A 15-minute agenda:

Bring to the table

  • 1–2 majors you're drawn to, and why — in one sentence each
  • Your research topic and how it points at that major
  • What that major can lead to (careers, graduate paths)

Ask your parents

  • What do you worry about with this path? 你担心什么？
  • What matters most — stability, passion, income, impact?
  • Can we pick a major that honors both our hopes?
If you disagree: a minor or a double interest is often the peace treaty — e.g. major in Economics (parents' comfort) + SML minor and an AI research topic (your passion). Coherent and reassuring.
Be honest that many families here have strong opinions about majors (often toward medicine, CS, finance). The frame that defuses conflict: majors aren't either/or, and a minor + an aimed research topic lets a student honor a parent's priority while keeping their own. Give them the bilingual questions so the conversation actually happens at home.
Quick check · the map

Quick check · tap an answer

At Princeton, which statement is TRUE?

Computer Science is the one field Princeton offers under both degrees — A.B. (Bachelor of Arts) and B.S.E. (Bachelor of Science in Engineering). Engineering majors earn a B.S.E., not a B.A.; concentrations are declared around the end of sophomore year, not at application; and the Statistics & Machine Learning minor is open to ANY major.

Reinforces the three factual pillars: two degrees, CS-in-both, and ML-is-a-minor-for-everyone. If students miss it, it's usually the 'declare before you apply' distractor — worth clarifying that the application signals a direction, it doesn't lock a concentration.
Assignment · 作业

Your "Major + Topic" one-pager

  1. Have the parent conversation using the agenda two slides back. Note one thing you agreed on and one worry they raised.
  2. Pick 1–2 target majors at a dream school (Princeton or any). Write the exact name and the division it's in.
  3. Aim your research topic: in 3–4 sentences, explain how your course project would be evidence for that major.
  4. Bonus: find that school's "fields of study" page and screenshot the 2–3 majors that fit you.

Wrap-up

Three things to keep

  • A university is a menu of majors — Princeton alone has 37 concentrations; your job is to find the 1–2 that fit you
  • AI is a method, not one major — carry it into biology, economics, psychology, language, physics; certify it with a minor like SML
  • Pick the major, aim the research, and your application tells one convincing story — said and shown
