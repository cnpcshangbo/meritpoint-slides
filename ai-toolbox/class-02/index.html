The Story

The race no car could finish

In 2004, DARPA offered one million dollars to any team whose car could drive itself 150 miles across the Mojave Desert. Not one car finished. The best managed 7.4 miles before getting stuck. People called it a failure. One year later — with the challenge sharpened and the teams focused on specific, measurable sub-problems — five cars finished the entire course. That second race launched the modern self-driving industry.
Neural voice · click to play / pause

2004's question was a wish: "can a car drive itself?" 2005's teams asked answerable questions — "can we keep GPS lock in a tunnel?", "can we tell a rock from a shadow at 30 mph?" — and the wish became engineering.

Tell it as drama: same teams, same desert, one year apart. What changed wasn't mainly the hardware — it was the questions. Vague ambition became a stack of specific, testable sub-problems. That is today's whole lesson in one story. Bridge: so what IS research, exactly?

Concept 1

What research actually is

Not just Googling

Searching finds existing answers. Research critically filters, evaluates, and integrates — it doesn't passively receive.

A systematic loop

Clear question, scientific method, reproducible validation, honest analysis — a closed loop, not random trial-and-error.

Creates new knowledge

The goal: knowledge or insight that didn't exist before and can withstand review by others.

1
Ask the questionfrom observation & curiosity
2
Form a hypothesisa testable expectation
3
Design the experimentvariables, samples, method
4
Evidence & analysiscollect data, conclude — or loop back
The three boxes kill the 'research = googling' myth. The four steps preview Class 3's full pipeline — today we live entirely inside step 1, because steps 2-4 inherit everything from it. The dashed step 4: results often send you back to sharpen the question.

Concept 2

What makes a question "researchable"

A research question is a single, answerable question that a study can settle with evidence.

  • Specific — names a scenario, not a sweeping theme
  • Measurable — you can collect data on it
  • Answerable — within your time, skills, and resources
🔍→📊

The picture test:
close your eyes and picture the table of data that would answer your question. Can't see it? Not a research question yet.

KEY IDEA — If you can't picture the data that would answer it, it's not a research question yet.
The picture test is the single most reusable tool of the hour — students will apply it in the labs and the project. Run it live: 'Is AI good?' → no imaginable table. 'Does night-photo training raise night recall?' → you can literally sketch the columns.
Concept 3 · the idea in one picture

The question ladder: vague → better → strong

  • Vague — "Is self-driving good?" Nothing here can be measured.
  • Better — "Can a model detect pedestrians at night?" Testable, but broad.
  • Strong — "Do low-light training images raise night recall without hurting day accuracy?"

Bad questions are vague, unfalsifiable, or already answered. Good ones are narrow enough to test, open enough to be interesting.

Takeaway: a strong question names what you'll measure and what would count as success. Notice the strong rung even guards against a side effect — "without hurting day accuracy."
The course's signature visual for question quality. Climb it aloud with the AV example. The trade-off clause in the strong rung is what separates student questions from publishable ones — improvement claims need a 'without breaking X' guard.
Concept 4 · from interest to AI problem

The AI-question recipe: input → output → metric

Abstracting turns a feeling into a model. Name three things and any interest becomes an AI question:

📥 Input

What the model sees — an image, a sentence, a row of sensor data.
Night camera frames.

📤 Output

What it predicts — a label, a number, a box.
"Pedestrian here" boxes.

📏 Metric

How you'll know it worked — one honest number.
Recall: share of real pedestrians found.

KEY IDEA — input + output + metric = an AI research question. This triple is the seed of every ML study you'll run in this course — and it's exactly what AI research questions demand: data-centric, model-building, quantifiable.
The operational recipe behind concept 2's picture test: the input/output pair IS the data table, the metric is its score column. Drill fast orally: essay grading (essay → grade → agreement with teachers), bird app (photo → species → top-1 accuracy). Students reuse this on every project slide for 13 more classes.
Check · question quality

Quick check · tap an answer

“Do low-light training images raise night-time pedestrian recall without hurting daytime accuracy?” — what makes this a STRONG research question?

Strong = specific data (low-light images), a measurable outcome (night recall), and a success condition with a guard (day accuracy must not drop). Excitement and length are irrelevant.

If students miss this, re-climb the ladder slide. The trade-off guard is the subtlest part — worth repeating.
Case study 1 · autonomous driving

"I want to make cars safer" — sharpened twice

A valuable vision, but too broad to act on. Real AV teams split it into layer-specific, measurable questions:

Perception layer

Q: How can a CNN detect pedestrians more accurately in low-light night conditions?
Input: night camera frames · Output: pedestrian boxes · Metric: accuracy & recall.

Prediction layer

Q: How can an RNN predict other vehicles' trajectories 5 seconds ahead more accurately?
Input: recent motion tracks · Output: future path · Metric: error vs. the real trajectory.

Same recipe both times: specific scenario + model + metric. Abstract safety became two testable studies — this is our running AV example wearing its research-question hat.
Connects to Class 1's perceive/predict/plan case study — each layer generates its own research questions. Note both questions pass the picture test: you can sketch the data table for each.
Case study 2 · medical imaging

"Use AI to help doctors" — sharpened twice

🫁 Lung nodule screening

Q: Can a deep model tell malignant from benign nodules in low-dose CT scans?
Metric: classification accuracy — while controlling false positives, so doctors aren't flooded with false alarms.

🧠 Brain tumor segmentation

Q: Can a U-Net segment MRI tumor regions pixel-precisely for surgery planning?
Metric: Dice coefficient — overlap between the model's region and expert annotation.

New field, same recipe. Notice each question carries its own honest metric — and a guard (false positives) just like the ladder's strong rung. Every domain you care about has questions shaped exactly like these.
Second domain proves the recipe generalizes. Dice and recall get proper treatment in later classes — today they're just 'the one honest number'. Ask: what would the input/output table look like for each? (CT slice → benign/malignant; MRI voxels → tumor mask.)
Finding your question · method 1

The funnel: interest → sub-field → pain point → AI question

1
Broad field"Environmental protection" — what you care about
2
Sub-field"Wildlife conservation" — where your interest sharpens
3
Real pain point"How to monitor endangered species without disturbing them?"
4
AI angle"Can computer vision count snow leopards in drone images?"
Result ▸ How can a YOLOv8 model detect and count snow leopards in complex mountainous drone images — despite vegetation, lighting changes, and camouflage?
Each step narrows: field → direction → problem → testable question. If your question still feels foggy, you stopped narrowing too early.

Finding your question · method 2

SCAMPER: seven angles on any process

Stuck? Point these seven prompts at any existing product or process — each angle can expose a research question.

S · Substitute

Can AI replace a manual process or tool?

C · Combine

Can AI + another technology create something new?

A · Adapt

Can a model from one field work in a totally different one?

M · Modify

Can we tune a model's structure or inputs to do better?

P · Put to other uses

What else could this existing AI system do?

E · Eliminate

Can AI remove a step, a cost, or a risk entirely?

R · Reverse — flip the roles or the order: instead of "librarians label books for readers," what if readers' AI tags label books for the library? The seventh angle is often the weirdest — and the most original.
Method 2 — for when the funnel stalls. Don't memorize the acronym; the skill is deliberately shifting angles instead of staring harder. R gets the callout both for layout (7th box) and because reversal questions are often the most novel. Concrete application next slide.
SCAMPER · applied to one boring place

Seven research questions hiding in a library

🤖 S

Can a robot classify and reshelve books as well as a librarian?

😊 C

Face recognition + borrowing system = "face-scan checkout"?

🛒 A

Can e-commerce "you may also like" tech recommend library books?

🗣️ M

Can search understand "that dragon book from last summer" — not just keywords?

📈 P

Can borrowing data predict community reading trends and guide purchasing?

🚫 E

Can AI sorting eliminate misplaced books at the source?

One quiet building, seven possible studies — and each still needs the recipe: what's the input, the output, the metric? (Try it: the recommender's would be borrow history → suggested titles → % of suggestions borrowed.)
Proof SCAMPER works on deliberately unglamorous settings — students don't need an exotic topic. Pick one box and run the recipe aloud with the class. R appears on the previous slide, so only six boxes here.
Check · SCAMPER

Quick check · tap an answer

Taking the 'you may also like' recommendation engine from online shopping and making it suggest library books is which SCAMPER move?

Adapt takes something that already works in one domain (e-commerce recommendations) and transplants it to another (libraries). Nothing is replaced or removed — the technique just moves house.

Common confusion is S vs. A: Substitute swaps out the doer of a task; Adapt transplants a technique across domains. If they get this, SCAMPER has landed.
Finding your question · method 3

Stand on shoulders: search the literature

Why read papers?

Status quo — what's been done. Gaps — what hasn't. Direction — where the field says "future work should…"

The tools

Google Scholar — search everything. Semantic Scholar & Connected Papers — see citation maps, find related work visually.

The 3-step loop

Anchor — find one good survey paper. Extend — follow its citation graph. Lock — read "Future Work" sections for unexplored directions.

The secret: the "Future Work" paragraph at the end of a paper is a list of research questions the authors couldn't get to — free, expert-approved gaps waiting for someone. Research gaps live at the boundary between known and unknown.

Method 3 — the professional route, demoed live shortly. High-schoolers CAN read abstracts and Future Work sections without following the math. Homework has them practice on 3 abstracts. Frame Scholar-finds-it-answered as good news: narrow it or add a twist.

Your project

Write three research questions

Take a topic you love — ideally from your Class-1 application map — and write three questions at rising quality: one simple, one medium, one strong.

  • Pick your topic (any domain — sport, music, climate, games…)
  • Write a simple, a medium, and a strong AI question
  • Label why each sits at its level — which ingredient is missing or present?

Deliverable

3 labeled research questions, each with a one-line justification.

Success looks like

The strong one names an input, an output, and a metric — and is answerable with data you could realistically get.

The deliverable from the curriculum: 3 questions at simple/medium/strong. Writing all three levels — not just the strong one — trains the ladder as a skill: they must know WHY the weak ones are weak. The justification line is where learning is visible; grade that, not topic glamour.

Draft, sharpen, and reality-check your questions

  3. Ask ChatGPT: "Give me 5 research questions about <topic>" — then sort them weak → strong
  4. Rewrite the best one in input → output → metric form

Benchmark · judge against this

What a finished strong question looks like

❌ Not research questions

"How can we make AI smarter?" — no domain, no data, no metric.

"Will AI replace humans?" — a debate topic; no experiment can settle it.

✅ A real one (student-reachable)

"Phishing emails keep mutating past old filters. Can an attention-based model beat a classic keyword filter on detection accuracy and F1 — especially on never-seen-before phishing styles?"

Specific domain

Phishing detection — not "cybersecurity" in general.

Honest metrics

Accuracy, F1 — and a hard test: unseen phishing styles.

Real value

A live security pain point — the answer would matter.

Calibration before wrap-up: measure your strong question against this bar. The 'never-seen-before styles' clause parallels the ladder's trade-off guard — strong questions bake the HARD test into the question itself. It passes the checker, the picture test, and the value test.
Reference · key terms

Today's vocabulary

Research question — a single, answerable question a study can settle with evidence.

Hypothesis — your testable expectation of the answer, made before looking.

Metric — the honest number that says how well it worked (accuracy, recall, error…).

Recall — of all the real positives out there, the share your model actually found.

Literature review — systematically reading prior work to map the known and unknown.

Research gap — a question at the edge of the known that nobody has answered yet.

Reference slide — point students back here whenever a term resurfaces. Recall gets a precise definition in the metrics class; today's version is deliberately intuitive and matches Lab 2's usage.

Backup · extended thinking

What makes a question worth asking?

🎓 Academic value

Does it fill a theoretical gap or push the boundary of what's known?

🌍 Practical value

Does it address a real social need or a genuine pain point someone has today?

💡 Innovation value

Does it bring a new method, a new angle, or a new framing to an old problem?

Self-check: score your strong question 1–5 on each dimension. It doesn't need three 5s — but three 1s means keep looking. Most great student projects score high on practical value first.

Backup · question first-aid

The four ways questions fail — and the fix

1 · Too broad

"How can AI improve education?" → Fix: run the funnel one more turn. Which subject? Which task? Which learners?

2 · Not measurable

"Can AI make music more beautiful?" → Fix: find the proxy metric. Listener ratings? Skip rate? Define the number.

3 · Already answered

Scholar's first page settles it → Fix: add a twist — new scenario, new data, stricter guard. ("…but does it hold for Mandarin emails?")

4 · Data out of reach

"…using hospital MRI records" you'll never access → Fix: hunt public datasets first (Kaggle, UCI, Hugging Face — or ScholarMind), then shape the question around what exists.

Diagnosis chart for peer review: every weak student question lands in one of these four bins, and each has a mechanical fix. Failure 4 is the sneakiest — perfect on paper, impossible in practice; that's why ScholarMind checks datasets up front.
