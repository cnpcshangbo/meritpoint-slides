The Story

They drove a city to build a benchmark

In 2012, researchers in Karlsruhe, Germany bolted cameras, lasers, and GPS onto a station wagon and drove it around the city for hours. The result — the KITTI dataset — became the testing ground that self-driving perception research used for the next decade. Thousands of papers were built on data from one rigged-up car driving real streets.
Neural voice · click to play / pause

Sound familiar? It's the ImageNet lesson (Class 3) on wheels: the field didn't advance because someone had a cleverer model — it advanced because someone collected and shared the right data. Datasets are research infrastructure.

Second dataset-origin story of the course, deliberately rhyming with ImageNet — students should say 'that's ImageNet again' and that's the point: data collection IS a research contribution, sometimes the biggest one. KITTI reappears in the case study.
Concept 1 · look around you

Everything is data (once you record it)

📏 Your height & weight?

Data. Quantified body measurements — numbers with units.

💬 A social-media post?

Data. Text carries opinions; likes and reposts record relationships.

📸 A phone photo?

Data. Millions of pixel values — a grid of numbers representing light.

KEY IDEA — data is a recorded symbol of something real: numbers, text, pixels, sounds. And AI's whole trick (Class 1!) is learning patterns from those symbols. No recording → no data → nothing to learn from. That's why researchers obsess over collection.
The icebreaker from the Lesson deck, compressed. Have students name one thing they did today that produced data (paying, walking past a camera, this deck logging quiz answers…). Everything downstream of this slide is about recorded symbols.

Concept 3 · the second axis

Quantitative vs. qualitative — the type picks the tools

🔢 Quantitative — "how much"

Discrete: counts — objects per frame, number of siblings.
Continuous: any value in a range — distance_m, temperature, confidence.

🏷️ Qualitative — "what kind"

Nominal: categories, no order — pedestrian / car / sign, blood type.
Ordinal: ordered, uneven gaps — grades A/B/C, satisfaction ★–★★★★★.

Why care? The type decides everything downstream: which chart (Class 9), which statistic (mean of nominal = nonsense), and which encoding a model needs (Class 8 turns "pedestrian" into numbers — carefully). Misread the type, and every later stage quietly breaks.
Drill fast: object_class? nominal. conf? continuous. objects-per-frame? discrete. star rating? ordinal — and the trap: it LOOKS numeric but 4★−3★ ≠ 2★−1★. The encoding teaser sets up Class 8's one-hot gotcha perfectly.
Check · classify it

Quick check · tap an answer

Your study-tracker table has a column 'focus level' recorded as ⭐ to ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐. What kind of data is that?

Star ratings are ordered (4★ > 2★) but the distance between levels isn't a fixed unit — that's ordinal. Treating it as a plain number (averaging stars) is common but sneaky: it assumes equal gaps that were never measured.

The subtlest of the four types, chosen deliberately — students' own life-datasets (Class 5 discussion) are full of ordinal columns. 'You can rank it but not subtract it' is the memorable test.
Concept 4 · the three wells

Where researchers actually get data

🏛️ Public datasets

Kaggle, Roboflow Universe, UCI, Hugging Face, government portals, academic benchmarks (KITTI!). Free, labeled, documented — start here, always.

🔐 Your own / proprietary

Data you collect (surveys, sensors, experiments) or a company's internal records. Highest relevance, highest cost — collection is a project in itself.

🌐 The open web

Crawlers scrape pages; APIs hand you structured data politely. Fresh and vast — but with rules (next slide).

For this course: public datasets are your well — your Class-5 skills work on them immediately, and their licenses are written down. Well-chosen public data beats painfully self-collected data for a first project, every time.
Set expectations: nobody is scraping or running surveys for their course project — public data is the sanctioned path (and what the auto-graded deliverable assumes). The web well gets its ethics slide next because students WILL try it someday.
The web well · two buckets, one rulebook

Crawlers scrape; APIs ask nicely

🕷️ Web crawler

A script that fetches pages and parses out the data (Python: requests + BeautifulSoup). Powerful — and easy to do rudely or illegally.

🔌 API

The site's official data door: get a key → read the docs → request → receive clean JSON. Stable, legal, structured — always prefer the API if one exists (weather, maps, social platforms…).

⚖️ The rulebook (non-negotiable): respect robots.txt · throttle your request rate · never collect personal or private information · check the site's terms and data copyright. A crawler that ignores these isn't research — it's trespassing with extra steps.
Awareness-level only — no crawling in this course, but the ethics block is the durable lesson. The API-first rule is professional practice. If asked 'can I scrape X for my project': the answer is 'does X have an API or an existing public dataset? use that.'
Concept 5 · judge before you trust

Every dataset has a license and a story

📜 The license

CC0 / CC-BY: use freely (credit for BY). Research-only: no commercial use. Unknown: treat as unusable. The license is usually one click away — read it.

📖 The collection story

Who collected it, when, where, how? KITTI = one car, one German city, daytime, 2012. That story defines what the data can — and cannot — say.

🕳️ The baked-in bias

KITTI's story means: German roads, good weather, 2012-era cars. A model trained on it has never seen a snowy night in Shanghai. Bias isn't a scandal — it's a documented limitation… if you look.

The instructor's rule: if the license is unclear — pick a different dataset. Don't guess. And write the collection story into your datasheet: it's tomorrow's "limitations" section (Class 4!) writing itself.
The hour's moral core. Bias framing matters: not gotcha-hunting but honest documentation — every dataset is a sample from somewhere, and knowing WHERE is what lets you claim honestly what your model can do. Ties to Class 3's ethics backup (bias = model-stage harm born at data stage).
Check · the judgment layer

Quick check · tap an answer

You find the perfect dataset for your project, but its page shows no license at all. What's the researcher's move?

No license = all rights reserved by default, not free-for-all. The professional moves are: find a licensed alternative (usually easy on Kaggle/Roboflow), or ask the author directly. Options B and D make it worse — hiding sources and license-laundering.

Counter-intuitive legal default worth stating plainly: absence of a license is maximum restriction, not zero restriction. The 'ask the author' path is real and often works — datasets have humans behind them.
Case study · our running example's data shelf

The datasets self-driving research runs on

🚗 KITTI / nuScenes

The classic benchmarks: camera + lidar + labeled boxes. KITTI (2012) started it; nuScenes added nights, rain, and 360° sensors — because KITTI's story lacked them.

🌊 Waymo Open

Industrial scale: ~2,000 hours, multi-sensor, many cities. Free for research — license explicitly non-commercial. Modern papers benchmark here.

📦 Roboflow Universe

Thousands of small, ready-to-train sets (including night-pedestrian ones!) — the practical shelf for a student project: download in YOLO format, train same-day.

Read the shelf like a researcher: each newer dataset exists to fix the previous one's collection-story gaps — nuScenes added the nights KITTI lacked. Dataset evolution IS the field saying "our data was biased; here's better." Prefer another domain? Same three-tier shelf exists everywhere.
The 'datasets fix each other's biases' arc is the deep point — it turns slide 12's bias concept into visible history. For student projects, Roboflow tier is the realistic one; KITTI/Waymo are for reading papers about (their Class-6 literature maps likely cite them).

Your project

Choose and document your dataset

Pick the dataset your project will actually use — and document it the way a researcher would, in a datasheet.

  • Browse Kaggle / Roboflow Universe for candidates matching your question
  • Check the license and the size — apply the judgment layer
  • Record the source and collection story (who, when, how)
  • Describe every variable: meaning, type (today's vocabulary!), units

Deliverable

A dataset datasheet: source, license, size, collection story, and a variable table.

Success looks like

License and source clearly stated — and every variable has a meaning and units.

The graded deliverable (Classroom: 'A chosen dataset with a documented variable sheet'). This is the real 'Datasheets for Datasets' practice, student-sized. The variable table doubles as their Class-8 cleaning worksheet — same columns, next week's mess.

What a finished datasheet looks like

Dataset: "Night-Time Pedestrians v2" (Roboflow Universe)

Source: universe.roboflow.com/… · License: CC-BY 4.0 ✓ · Size: 1,847 images, 4,210 boxes

Collection story: dashcam footage, 2 US cities, Nov–Feb, dusk-to-night only; boxes drawn by the uploading team.

Variables: image (unstructured, 640×640 px) · class (nominal: pedestrian/cyclist) · x,y,w,h (continuous, 0–1 normalized box) · lighting (ordinal: dusk/night/deep-night)

Known limitations (from the story): US cities only · winter clothing bias · no rain or snow nights · single labeling team, no cross-check. Claims about other conditions are extrapolation.

The bar: every field filled from evidence, variable types named with today's vocabulary, and the limitation box derived from the collection story — not guessed. This page is the first section of your final paper's "Data" chapter, already written.
The limitations-derived-from-story move is the sophistication marker — bias documented as geography/season/process facts, not vague worry. The 'final paper chapter' framing shows this work compounds into Class 15.
Reference · key terms

Today's vocabulary

Structured / unstructured — fits a table now vs. needs a model (perception) to get there.

Quantitative (discrete / continuous) — "how much": counts vs. measurements.

Qualitative (nominal / ordinal) — "what kind": unordered categories vs. ordered levels.

License (CC0 / CC-BY / research-only) — the legal terms of use; unknown = unusable.

API — a site's official structured-data door; prefer it over scraping.

Datasheet — a dataset's documentation: source, license, collection story, variables, limitations.

'Datasheet' as a term comes from Gebru et al.'s 'Datasheets for Datasets' — worth a name-drop for advanced students. The four data-type words return constantly in Classes 8-10.

Backup · the rulebook, with reasons

Why the crawler rules exist

robots.txt & rate limits

The site's stated rules + not overwhelming their servers. Ignoring them can take a small site down — that's harm, not research.

Privacy

Public ≠ fair game: names, faces, and posts belong to people. Scraped personal data can't be "un-collected" — the harm outlives the project.

Copyright & terms

Text, images, and databases have owners; terms of service are agreements. "For research" is not a universal exemption.

The honest test

Would you be comfortable telling the site's owner — and the people in the data — exactly what you collected and why? If not, stop.

Reasons, not just rules — teenagers respect 'here's who gets hurt' over 'it's forbidden'. The honest test generalizes to all data ethics and echoes Class 3's ethics backup.
