The Story

The dataset that woke up AI

For decades, researchers tweaked algorithms and stayed stuck. In 2009, Fei-Fei Li bet on the opposite idea: maybe the bottleneck was data, not models. Her team labeled millions of images by hand to build ImageNet. In 2012, a neural network trained on it crushed every competitor — and the deep-learning era began. The breakthrough wasn't a cleverer model. It was the data stage of the pipeline.
Neural voice · click to play / pause

Remember Class 1's history: the 2010s boom came from data + compute, not just cleverer math. ImageNet is that lesson with a name and a face.

Tell it as a bet that paid off: everyone optimized one pipeline stage (model) while the real bottleneck was another (data). This seeds pitfall #1 later — spend half your time on data — and connects to Class 1's history slide.
Concept 1 · the spine of this course

Data → Model → Experiment → Conclusion

Every ML study — a student project or a billion-dollar lab — walks the same four stages:

1
DataExamples to learn from — gathered, cleaned, split.
2
ModelThe thing that learns a pattern from the examples.
3
ExperimentA fair measurement on data the model hasn't seen.
4
ConclusionOnly what the numbers support — no more.

Step 1 of 5 · problem definition

Decide what game you're playing

🎯 Task type

Is the output a category (classification), a number (regression), or groups with no labels (clustering)? This one choice picks your models and metrics.

📏 Success metric

Classification → accuracy, recall, F1. Regression → error (MSE/MAE). Set the target number up front: "accuracy ≥ 95% = success."

🧱 Scope

What data can you actually get? What hardware and time do you have? Boundaries make the project finishable.

Sound familiar? This is Class 2's input → output → metric recipe, now wearing engineering clothes. Your strong question already did step 1 — that's why we wrote it first.
The explicit bridge from Class 2: a strong research question IS a completed step 1. Drill the task-type question orally: pedestrian boxes (classification+detection), house price (regression), grouping students by study style (clustering).
Step 2 of 5 · data acquisition & preprocessing

"Garbage in, garbage out"

📥 Get the data

Public: Kaggle, UCI, Hugging Face — free, labeled, ready. Your own: sensors, forms, photos, scraping. Most student projects should start public — collection is a project in itself.

🧹 Prep the data

Clean: fix noise, fill or drop missing values. Engineer features: pick and transform the useful columns. Split: train / validation / test — three piles with three different jobs.

Why it's step 2 and not an afterthought: the model can only ever be as good as what it eats. ImageNet won 2012 at this step, not step 3 — and pitfall #1 (coming up) says spend half your time here.
GIGO is the slide's soul. The three-pile split gets fully explained in step 4/5 and lab 2 — here just name it. Data classes 7-9 expand every phrase on this slide.
Step 3 of 5 · model construction & training

Pick a learner, let it learn, tune the knobs

🗡️ Select

Match the tool to the task: simple problems → linear regression, decision trees. Messy, complex data → random forests, neural networks. Start simple — always.

🏋️ Train

Feed the training pile to the model. Through many iterations it learns the mapping from features to answers — exactly Class 1's "learn from data, not rules."

🎛️ Tune

Models have knobs — learning rate, tree depth. Adjust, re-measure, repeat. Like tuning an instrument: small turns, listen each time.

Classes 10–13 live entirely inside this box: regression → trees → forests → neural nets, each one hands-on.
Keep this light — four full classes cover it later. The 'start simple' seed grows into pitfall #2 (baseline first). Lab 1 today lets everyone actually train a model in the browser.
Step 4 of 5 · evaluation & optimization

Diagnose like a doctor: two classic diseases

🥶 Underfitting — too simple

The model can't even learn the training data. Symptom: bad scores everywhere.
Treatment: a more capable model, or better features.

🤒 Overfitting — too complex

The model memorized the training data, noise and all. Symptom: great on training data, bad on new data.
Treatment: more data, regularization, or a simpler model.

The tell: always compare training score vs. unseen-data score. A big gap = overfitting. You'll see this live in Lab 2 — a model that scores perfectly on what it saw and falls apart on what it didn't.
The most important new vocabulary of the day. The doctor framing keeps it memorable: symptom → diagnosis → treatment. Lab 2 makes the train-vs-test gap tangible with polynomial degrees.
Step 5 of 5 · final test & presentation

The final exam — then tell the world

🔒 Final test

One last measurement on the test set — data the model has never seen, not even during tuning. This is the honest, unbiased answer to your research question. Used once.

📣 Present

Paper, report, slides, or blog: problem → method → results → conclusion → future work. Class 2's tip: that "future work" section is where someone else's next question comes from — now you're writing one.

Golden rule: the test set is sacred. Tune on it even once and its score becomes a lie — that's "data leakage," pitfall #3. Train on the train pile, tune on the validation pile, touch the test pile once.
The three-pile discipline lands here fully. 'Future work' bridges back to Class 2's literature method — students now see both sides of that paragraph. Presentation gets its own class (15).

Concept 3 · why the pipeline looks like this

Rules in — or rules out?

Traditional programming

data + rules → answers

A human writes every rule by hand. The computer just executes. (Deep Blue, calculators, thermostats.)

Machine learning

data + answers → rules

Show examples with known answers; the machine infers the rules itself — including rules no human could write down.

This is why data is a pipeline stage at all: in ML, data isn't fuel for a program — data is the program's teacher. The essence of AI research: automatically discovering patterns in data that humans can't perceive.
One-line inversion: rules are an input in traditional programming, an OUTPUT in ML. Connects Class 1's Deep-Blue-vs-ChatGPT distinction to today's pipeline: no data stage, nothing to learn from.
Concept 4 · two ways to learn

Supervised vs. unsupervised

👩‍🏫 Supervised — with answer key

Every example comes labeled: this image is a pedestrian, this house sold for $340k. The model learns to map input → label.
Classification and regression both live here.

🧭 Unsupervised — no answer key

No labels at all. The model finds structure on its own: clusters of similar customers, groups of similar songs.
Clustering is the classic example.

Our running example is supervised: pedestrian detection learns from boxes humans drew — that hand-labeling is exactly what made ImageNet valuable, and it's why the data stage costs real effort. Most of this course is supervised learning.
Tie all three threads: ImageNet story (labels cost millions of hours), AV case (who drew the boxes?), and course scope (mostly supervised). Quiz next.
Check · two ways to learn

Quick check · tap an answer

You have 10,000 customer records and want to discover natural groups of similar shoppers — no predefined categories. Which kind of learning is this?

No predefined answer to learn from = unsupervised. If instead you wanted to predict 'will this customer buy again? yes/no' with historical answers, THAT would be supervised classification.

The explanation's contrast (same data, different question → different paradigm) is the deeper lesson: the QUESTION picks the paradigm, echoing step 1.

Benchmark · judge against this

What a finished mini-plan looks like

Example question: "Can a model tell cats from dogs in photos?"

1 · Problem: binary image classification — photo in, cat/dog out.

2 · Success metric: accuracy ≥ 95% on unseen photos (F1 as backup).

3 · Data plan: Kaggle cats-vs-dogs dataset; resize all images; augment; split 70/10/20.

4 · Model path: baseline = small CNN → then fine-tune a pretrained ResNet50.

5 · Evaluation: tune on the validation pile only; touch the test pile once, at the end.

⚠ Predicted hard part: data — photos vary wildly in lighting and pose; augmentation will matter.

Every line is concrete: a named dataset, a number to beat, a model path from simple to strong, an evaluation discipline. Measure your plan against this bar.

Field guide · how projects actually die

Four classic pitfalls (and the fix)

1 · Worshipping models, ignoring data

Weeks on a fancy model fed garbage data. Fix: spend ~half your time on understanding and cleaning data. (ImageNet's whole lesson.)

2 · Starting with the fanciest model

Jumping straight to the deep end. Fix: baseline first — a simple model that sets the bar and reveals how hard the problem really is.

3 · Grading on the training data

"99% accuracy!" …on data it memorized. Fix: the three piles. Report only unseen-data scores; touch the test set once.

4 · Numbers with no picture

A wall of metrics nobody understands. Fix: visualize — one honest chart beats a table of digits. (Class 9 is all about this.)

Each pitfall now has hooks: #1 ImageNet, #2 the plan template's baseline line, #3 quiz-overfitting's trap option, #4 forward to Class 9. Frame as 'the four ways your project will TRY to die — now you know the vaccines.'
Reference · key terms

Today's vocabulary

Pipeline — the loop every study walks: data → model → experiment → conclusion.

Supervised / unsupervised — learning with labeled answers vs. finding structure without labels.

Train / validation / test sets — three piles: learn from, tune on, final-exam once.

Baseline — the simple model you build first; everything fancier must beat it.

Underfitting / overfitting — too simple to learn vs. memorized the noise; diagnose via the train-vs-test gap.

Data leakage — test information sneaking into training or tuning; makes scores lie.

Backup · extended thinking

The pipeline has a conscience: research ethics

🔐 Data privacy

Is your data legally sourced and properly authorized? Faces, health records, chat logs — being available doesn't make them fair game. (A data-stage question.)

⚖️ Algorithmic bias

If the training data under-represents a group, the model fails them — a detector trained mostly on daytime images misses at night. (A model-stage question.)

🌍 Social impact

Could your results be misused, or widen inequality? Anticipating harm is part of the conclusion stage — not someone else's job.

"As researchers we are not only creators of technology but bearers of responsibility." Notice each concern maps to a pipeline stage — ethics isn't a separate box, it rides inside every box.
The stage-mapping makes ethics concrete rather than preachy. The bias example deliberately reuses the day/night imbalance — the SAME fact that motivated the strong question is also a fairness issue.
Backup · the split, illustrated

Three piles, three jobs

📚 Training set (~70%)

The textbook. The model learns from this pile — it may read it as many times as it likes.

📝 Validation set (~10%)

The practice quiz. You tune knobs against this pile — compare models, pick settings.

🔒 Test set (~20%)

The final exam, sealed until the end. Opened once. Its score is the number you report.

Why so strict? Anything you optimize against, you slowly memorize — a student who practices on the final exam hasn't learned, just leaked. Same for models: that's data leakage.
The student-exam analogy is the strongest one available — use it if the piles confused anyone in step 5 or lab 2. Classic split 70/10/20 (the Lesson-3 deck's 7:1:2).
Backup · bonus check

Quick check · tap an answer

A researcher tries 50 different model settings, each time checking the score on the test set, and reports the best one. What went wrong?

Tuning is fine — against the VALIDATION pile. By checking the test set 50 times they optimized for it, so the reported score is quietly inflated. The test set answers honestly only if you ask it once.

The subtlest form of pitfall #3 — leakage without touching training. If students get this one, the three-pile discipline has truly landed.
